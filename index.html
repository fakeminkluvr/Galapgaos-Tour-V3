Part One

Charles Darwin & the Voyage of HMS Beagle

In 1831, a 22-year-old Charles Darwin was offered the position of naturalist aboard HMS Beagle — a Royal Navy survey ship heading around the world. The voyage lasted nearly five years and would ultimately reshape how we understand life on Earth.

Darwin spent this time observing and collecting specimens across South America, the Pacific, and beyond. His observations challenged the idea that species were fixed and unchanging.

Plymouth, England → Cape Verde → Brazil → Argentina → Chile → Galápagos ★ → Tahiti → New Zealand → Australia → England

HMS Beagle arrived at the Galápagos on 15 September 1835. Darwin spent approximately five weeks across four islands — San Cristóbal, Floreana, Isabela, and Santiago — before departing on 20 October 1835.

Key observations in the Galápagos

San Cristóbal

Tortoises with different shell shapes

The local vice-governor could tell which island a tortoise was from by its shell shape — dome-shaped on humid islands, saddle-backed on dry ones. Darwin initially dismissed this, but it would prove critical.

Floreana & other islands

Mockingbirds differ between islands

Darwin collected mockingbirds from different islands and noticed they were slightly but consistently different — an early hint at island-specific variation.

Multiple islands

Finches with varied beak shapes

Darwin collected around 70 bird specimens including 13 finch species (he didn't realise their significance at the time). Back in London, ornithologist John Gould identified them all as finches — each adapted to a different food source. This was pivotal.

Marine environments

Marine iguanas: unique to the Galápagos

Darwin observed iguanas that had evolved to swim and feed on underwater algae — something no other lizard does. They illustrated how dramatically populations could adapt to local conditions.

How Darwin developed his theory

Darwin didn't have a "eureka" moment on the islands — the theory developed gradually. After returning to England in 1836, Gould's analysis of the finches was a turning point. Darwin also drew on Thomas Malthus's 1798 essay on population, which argued that populations grow faster than resources allow, causing competition. Darwin applied this to nature: those with heritable advantageous traits survive and reproduce, gradually changing a population over time.

Darwin worked on his theory for over 20 years before publishing On the Origin of Species in 1859.

The process of natural selection

1

Variation

Individuals show heritable variation in their traits — the raw material for evolution.

2

Overproduction

Populations produce more offspring than can possibly survive given limited resources.

3

Competition

Individuals compete with each other and other species for survival and reproduction.

4

Differential Survival

Those with advantageous traits survive and pass them on, gradually shifting the population.

Part Two

A Three-Island Educational Tour

Our tour visits three of the most biologically significant islands in the Galápagos. Each showcases a distinct set of adaptations and habitats that bring evolution to life.

Duration: 9 days  |  Route: Santa Cruz → Española → Isabela

Santa Cruz

Indefatigable Island

📍 Central Galápagos; most accessible

🌿 Multiple vegetation zones

📅 Days 1–3

🏛️ Charles Darwin Research Station

Why Santa Cruz?

Biological justification Highest biodiversity of any Galápagos island, spanning multiple vegetation zones from dry coastal scrub to lush highland forests. Also home to the tortoise breeding programme — essential for understanding conservation in the context of evolution.

Visitors observe giant tortoises in their natural highland habitat and learn from scientists at the research station about ongoing conservation and evolutionary research.

Galápagos Giant Tortoise

Chelonoidis niger

Structural Physiological Behavioural

Dome-shaped shell: Rounded shells on humid islands; saddle-backed on dry islands — neck stretches up to reach cacti. A direct example of natural selection acting on morphology.

Slow metabolism: Can survive up to a year without food or water by storing fat and water in tissues.

Vertical migration: Moves between cool highlands (wet season) and warm lowlands (dry season) to regulate temperature and access food.

Prickly Pear Cactus

Opuntia echios

Structural Physiological

Trunk-forming growth: On islands with tortoises and iguanas, these cacti evolved to grow tall with a woody trunk, raising edible pads out of reach. A fascinating example of co-evolution.

CAM photosynthesis: Opens stomata at night rather than day, dramatically reducing water loss in the dry climate.

Española

Hood Island

📍 Southernmost; oldest island

🦅 Highest endemism rate in Galápagos

📅 Days 4–6

🌊 Rocky coasts & seabird colonies

Why Española?

Biological justification The oldest island in the archipelago, geographically isolated for millions of years — resulting in the highest rate of endemic species of any Galápagos island. An exceptional case study in allopatric speciation.

Española demonstrates what happens when populations are isolated long enough: they diverge so significantly from their ancestors that they become entirely new species.

Waved Albatross

Phoebastria irrorata

Structural Behavioural

Wingspan up to 2.4 m: The largest wingspan in the Galápagos allows soaring over ocean for months, using wind gradients to travel vast distances with minimal energy.

Elaborate courtship rituals: Synchronised dances, bill clacking and sky-pointing form monogamous pair bonds — ensuring mating with a healthy, compatible partner.

Marine Iguana

Amblyrhynchus cristatus

Structural Physiological Behavioural

Flattened tail & blunt snout: The tail acts as a rudder for swimming; the blunt snout scrapes algae off submerged rocks — the only lizard in the world to feed in the ocean.

Salt-excreting nasal glands: Specialised glands excrete excess salt through the nostrils.

Communal basking: Group basking on lava rocks reduces heat loss after cold dives.

Isabela

Albemarle Island

📍 Largest island; over half the land area

🌋 Five active shield volcanoes

📅 Days 7–9

🐧 Only island where penguins breed

Why Isabela?

Biological justification The largest and most volcanically active island. Its five volcanoes are effectively isolated by lava flows, each supporting different tortoise subspecies — a miniature model of the entire Galápagos. Fresh lava flows allow us to study colonisation and early evolution in real time.

The island's geology is as important as its biology — lava flows represent "blank slates" for colonisation, directly relevant to understanding how evolution begins on isolated islands.

Galápagos Penguin

Spheniscus mendiculus

Structural Physiological Behavioural

Reduced feather insulation: Unlike Antarctic penguins, Galápagos penguins have thinner feathers — an adaptation to prevent overheating in a tropical climate. A remarkable example of structural adaptation driven by abiotic temperature factors.

Timed moult: Feather replacement coincides with the Humboldt Current — physiological cycles regulated by abiotic ocean conditions.

Panting & shade-seeking: Flippers held away from the body to increase heat loss on hot days.

Flightless Cormorant

Nannopterum harrisi

Structural Behavioural

Vestigial wings: The only cormorant that cannot fly. With no land predators on Isabela, maintaining large flight muscles was costly — so over thousands of generations, wings became smaller. This illustrates "relaxed selection."

Spread-wing drying posture: Despite not flying, cormorants still hold wings out to dry after diving — a retained ancestral behaviour.

Part Three

The Grants' Study: Evolution in Real Time

Peter and Rosemary Grant from Princeton University conducted one of the most important long-running studies in evolutionary biology. Beginning in 1973, they spent decades on Daphne Major — a tiny volcanic island — studying Darwin's finches. Their work provided the first direct, quantitative evidence of natural selection occurring in a wild population.

40+ Years of continuous study
18,000+ Individual birds banded & tracked
0.5 mm Average beak depth increase after 1977 drought

Key findings

The 1977 drought (abiotic factor): A severe drought eliminated most small, soft seeds — the preferred food of medium ground finches. Only large, hard seeds remained. Finches with larger, deeper beaks survived at higher rates. Average beak depth increased by ~0.5 mm in a single generation — measurable evolution driven by an abiotic factor (rainfall deficit).

The 1983 El Niño reversal (biotic factor): Heavy rain brought abundant small seeds. Now smaller-beaked finches had an advantage. Beak size shifted back — demonstrating that natural selection can reverse direction depending on changing conditions.

Competition between species (biotic factor): In 2004, drought coincided with the arrival of a larger finch species, Geospiza magnirostris. Large-beaked G. fortis were outcompeted for large seeds, so selection now favoured smaller beaks — showing how biotic factors (competition) drive natural selection just as powerfully as abiotic ones.

Why the Grants' study matters

Before the Grants' work, evolution was widely assumed to be too slow to observe directly. Their study proved this wrong. By measuring finch populations quantitatively over decades, they showed that natural selection can produce measurable evolutionary changes in just a few years, given strong enough selection pressure.
